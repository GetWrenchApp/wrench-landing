FAQ

Common questions

How do I know if I'm charging enough for a job? +

Add up your total labor cost (your hours × your hourly target + employee hours × their cost) plus materials. That's your break-even floor — your minimum price. A healthy quote is 8–15% above that to cover callbacks, scope creep, and estimation errors. If your quote is below that floor, you're paying to do the job.

What should my hourly rate be as a contractor? +

Most solo contractors need $65–$110/hr in revenue to actually profit after taxes, insurance, vehicle costs, and overhead. Calculate yours: add up all monthly business expenses, divide by average billable hours per month. That's your break-even rate — your target should be above it. Don't just charge what competitors charge; charge what your numbers require.

What is a quote buffer and why does it matter? +

A quote buffer is a percentage added above your minimum price — typically 8–15% — to protect against jobs running long, small scope changes, a callback, or materials costing more than expected. Without a buffer, any surprise turns a profitable job into a losing one. Think of it as built-in insurance on every estimate.

How do I calculate revenue per labor hour? +

Revenue per labor hour = (quote price − material cost) ÷ total labor hours. This strips out materials and shows how much you're actually earning per hour of work. Compare it to your required hourly rate to know instantly if the job is worth taking — or if you need to re-quote.
